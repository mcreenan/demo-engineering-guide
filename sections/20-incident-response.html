An incident is anything customer-affecting in production. This section owns the process once a page is acknowledged: classify the severity, assign roles, mitigate first (often a rollback), communicate on a cadence, resolve, and run a blameless postmortem for every Sev 1 or Sev 2.

In shortAcknowledge the page, declare in #incidents with a one-line summary and severity, look at the gateway, and check the most recent change — most incidents follow a deploy, so roll it back before deeper debugging. One person wears all the hats for a small incident; split Incident Commander, Responders, and Comms for a Sev 1. Every Sev 1/2 gets a blameless postmortem within two business days, with action items tracked to done.

The paging trigger and first triage steps belong to Observability & On-Call; this section starts where a page is acknowledged. The first-five-minutes flow comes from the runbook in (runbooks/incident-response.md).

Severity decides how loud the response is. Set it when you declare; raise it later if the picture worsens.

Roles

Incident management (wiki article 1006)

An incident has three roles. One person can wear all of them for a small incident; split them for a Sev 1 so coordination, hands-on work, and communication don’t compete for the same attention.

Incident Commander
Runs the incident, makes the calls, and keeps the timeline.
Responders
Do the hands-on work the Incident Commander coordinates.
Comms
Update #incidents and, for Sev 1/2, the status page.

The first five minutes

runbooks/incident-response.md Incident management (wiki article 1006)

When a page fires, work the runbook in order before reaching for anything clever:

  1. Acknowledge the page. Stop it escalating so the response can focus.
  2. Declare in #incidents. Post a one-line summary and the severity.
  3. Look at the gateway first. It is the single ingress, so most symptoms show there — see The Gateway & Request Flow.
  4. Check the recent change. Most incidents follow a deploy; if one is in flight or just finished, roll it back before deeper debugging.

The gateway commands are the same first look used in on-call triage:

# the gateway is the single place to look first
kubectl get pods -l tier=edge
kubectl logs -l app=gateway --tail=100

The flow

Incident management (wiki article 1006) runbooks/incident-response.md

The shape of every incident is the same: declare, mitigate first, communicate on a cadence, resolve, postmortem. Mitigation comes before root cause — stop the customer impact first, understand it after. Because most incidents follow a deploy, the first mitigation to reach for is a rollback.

The runbook gives no specific cadence interval — communicate “on a cadence,” keeping #incidents and, for Sev 1/2, the status page current. Comms owns those updates.

Mitigation: roll the deploy back

runbooks/incident-response.md Release & deployment (wiki article 1003)

A bad deploy is the most common cause, and a rollback resolves most Sev 1/2s. The mechanics live in Release & Deployment — in short, a rollback is a one-click redeploy of the previous image tag:

kubectl rollout undo deploy/gateway
Never hand-edit productionDo not patch a live deployment with kubectl edit — all production change goes through git. Roll back to the previous image instead. For the kubectl and EKS context, see Infrastructure & Environments.

Postmortems

Incident management (wiki article 1006) runbooks/incident-response.md

Every Sev 1 and Sev 2 gets a blameless postmortem within two business days, focused on the systemic fix rather than who did what. Action items are tracked in the wiki and followed to done — a postmortem with open action items isn’t finished.

Use the wiki templateThe runbook points to the “Incident management” wiki page (article 1006) for the postmortem template and process — start from it rather than a blank doc.
